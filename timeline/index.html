Overview

Below is a representation of the 2014 Ebola outbreak in western Africa. The lines infer disease transmission from one individual to another. Each small icon represents one case of the disease. As the outbreak spreads to many people, we represent multiple infections with larger icons.

The transmission links are only random estimates. True tracing of individual transmission is one of the great challenges for controlling the outbreak and may not be possible. Click the arrows at the left and right to learn more about the outbreak of the disease, or use the buttons below to filter and explore the data.

Origin

The outbreak was discovered upon first reports on March 23, 2014 in Guinea, where around 50 cases were suspected to exist and the outbreak was well underway. It is now known that a Guinean 2-year-old is the first known victim of Ebola who became sick in December 2013.

According to research models accounting for rate of disease transmission, this matches estimates that the initial case may have been 60 to 90 days prior to the first reports. It is thought that the most likely cause was transmission of the disease from bats to humans through consuming bat meat or contact with bat excrement.

Ebola spreads through Africa

From it's start in Guinea, Ebola began appearing in neighboring Sierra Leone and Liberia in May and June.

The pace quickens

We are now plotting cases across time in a cumulative case count. In this time period, we see that cases in Guinea did not grow rapidly, but outbreaks in Sierra Leone and Liberia contributed to a renewal of Ebola's rapid expansion.

Dire conditions

Back in the outbreak connections view, we see that August and September saw explosive growth of the disease in Guinea, Sierra Leone, and Liberia as health care facilities were stretched past their limits.

Nigeria (green) also saw a small outbreak, but it has since been contained, and later it was confirmed that the outbreak was over in that country after a 21-day waiting period with no new Ebola cases appearing.

Liberia becomes the main concern

Plotting these cumulative cases shows that though the outbreak began in Guinea, Liberia quickly outpaced all other countries and became the most dangerous place for Ebola.

Ebola travels

A small number of travel-related cases have been reported in Senegal, Spain, and the US, with the only known transmission occuring in the US, where two health care workers were infected.
